Nikola Tesla

Father of the Electric Age

Engineer, Inventor

Time Line of Nikola Tesla's Life

  • 10 July 1856 - Nikola Tesla was born to a Serbian family in Smilijan near Gospic, Lika (the Military Frontier of Austria-Hungary, now in Croatia).
  • 1862 - Tesla's father received an appointment as pastor to a church in Gospic, where the family moved. And Tesla completed "Lower" or "Primary" school. Then he attended "Lower Real Gymnasium" or "Normal School" followed by attendance to the "Real Gymnasium."
  • 1863 - Tesla's older and only brother died at the age of 12, after falling from a horse. Some accounts indicated, Tesla may have spooked the horse, which caused the accident.
  • 1870 - Tesla moved to Karlovac (Carlstadt), Croatia. He stayed with his aunt and Colonel "Old War-Horse" Brankovic. Here he attended "Higher Real Gymnasium" school, where teacher, Martin Sekulic taught him Mathematics and Physics. This had a major influence on him. Also, he graduated from school a year early.
  • 1873 - In spite of his father's instructions otherwise, Tesla returned to Gospic. Shortly after arriving, he contracted cholera. He was bedridden for 9 months and was near death. In a moment of desperation, Tesla's father agreed to send him to an engineering school, if he recovered from the illness. Tesla's father was a Serbian Orthodox Priest and writer, and he pushed his son to join the priesthood. But Tesla's interest was in Electrical Engineering, which was spurred by his mother, who in her spare time invented small applicances around the house.
  • 1874 - Tesla went to Tomingaj near Graeac to recover from cholera. He avoided the Army, from which he was a fugitive, due to the required three-years compulsory military service. Tesla later stated , "For most of this term I roamed in the mountains, loaded with a hunter's outfit and a bundle of books, and this contact with nature made me stronger in body as well as in mind."
  • 1875 - Tesla enrolled at the Austrian Polytechnic School in Graz on a Military Border Scholarship. In his first year, he never missed a lecture, made the highest grades possible, and started a Serbian culture club. During his second year, Tesla came into conflict with Professor Poeschl over the Gramme Dynamo, when he suggested that commutators were not necessary. At the end of his second year, Tesla lost his scholarship and took up gambling. Near the end of his third year, Tesla stopped attending lectures and dropped out the following year.
  • 1878 - The reason Tesla left Austria is unclear; nevertheless, his next stop was Maribor, Slovenia. He took his first job as a draftsman for a local engineering firm. He spent his spare time in the pub, where he played chess and cards.
  • 1879 - In March, Tesla's father came to Maribor and pleaded with him to return home, but Telsa refused. Tesla was reported the Maribor Police, because he did not have a residence permit. He was deported to Gospic. In April, Telsa's father died from an unspecified illness. Later in the year in Gospic, Tesla began teaching at his old "Real Gymnasium". He did not want this to be his future.
  • 1880 - Tesla's uncles came to his rescue and put together enough money to help him escape from Gospic. He travelled to Prague, but arrived too late to enroll in Karl-Ferdinand University. Even if he had arrived early enough, he would not have been permitted to enroll, because he had never studied Greek and he did not speak or write Czech. He spent most of his time at the Klementinum Library and Narodni Kavarna (People's Cafe). He did attend lectures at the University as an auditor, but did not receive marks for the courses.
  • 1881 - Tesla's uncle arranged for him to work for Ferenc Puskas at the Budapest Telephone Exchange in Hungary, which was then under construction. When Tesla arrived, he found that the new business was not anywhere near operational. Instead he worked as a draftsman in the Central Telegraph Office. Within a few months, the Budapest Telephone Exchange became operational and he was assigned the position of Chief Electrian. During his employment, Tesla made numerous improvements to the equipment and developed an amplifier device.
  • 1882 - In February, Tesla was obsessed with solving the riddle of Alternating Current, suffered a mental breakdown, and is not expected to live. A short time later, he begans to recover. And during a walk in the Varosliget City Park of Budapest with a friend, the solution comes to him in a vision. In April, Tesla and his friend accepted position with the Continental Edison Company, which had recently started in Paris. Tesla worked for Charles Batchelor at the Ivry-sur-Seine Lamp Factory. He installed lighting systems in the Paris Opera House and a theater in Bavaria. He submitted a plan for improving the Edison Dynamos to Mr. Rau, an administrator for the company. The plan was approved and soon Tesla's automatic regulator was accepted. Tesla was likely expecting to be compensated, he was dispatched to Strasbourg before payment was awarded.
  • 1883 - In February on assignment by the Continental Edison Company, Tesla was sent to Strasbourg, France to repair a new Direct Current lighting system installed at the German Railway company, which was damaged on a trial run. The government approved the job approved the job after the repairs, but Tesla never received the compensation he was promised for the work. In June, he demonstrated the newly completed Alternating Current induction motor before Mr. Bauzin, former mayor of Strasbourg, and several potential investors. They watched with interest, but it was apparent they did not understand the value of the invention.
  • 1884 - In the spring, Tesla's Uncles provided him the funds. He packed his gear and caught the next ship for America. His trip did not start too smoothly, because his ticket, money, and some of his luggage were stolen. To make matters worse, a mutiny broke out on the ship, and Tesla was almost thrown overboard. A few weeks later, he arrived in New York with four cents in his pocket, a few poems, and remnants of his belongings. Shortly after arriving in New York, Thomas Edison essentially hired Telsa on the spot, but would hear nothing of his Alternating Current Power System.
  • 1885 - In March, investors approached Tesla and asked him to develop an improved arc lighting system. Although this was not the opportunity he had hoped for, the group was willing to finance the Tesla Electric Light Company in Rahway, New Jersey. The proud new owner set to work and invented a unique arc lamp of beautiful design and efficiency. After completing the work, Tesla was forced out of the company and left with nothing but worthless stock certifcates. Tesla later referred to this as the "the hardest blow" he had ever received. At some point during Tesla's employment, Edison promised to pay him $50,000, if he could improve the performance of Edison's Direct Current Dynamo. Telsa succeeded beyond Edison expectations, but Edison reneged on his promise. Edison is quoted as stating to Tesla, "You don't understand our American humor." Deeply hurt, Tesla resigned. Some accounts state that Edison offered Telsa a raised if he would reconsider, but Tesla declined.
  • 1886 - Tesla felted betrayed by men he trusted. He considered the winter from 1886 to 1887 a time of "terrible headaches and bitter tears." He was forced to work as a ditch digger for $2.00 per day, and he questioned the value of his education.
  • 1887 - Tesla was introduced to Alfred Brown, Director of Western Union, and Charles Peck, a New York City Attorney. After he demonstrated his "Egg of Columbus", they were sold on his Alternating Current System. Tesla agreed to split the royalities from his patents on a fifty-fifty basis in exchange for funding. Alfred Brown located the laboratory at 89 Liberty Street, and the company filed for its first patent by the end of the month. In May, Tesla's oldest and best friend, Anthony Stzigeti, arrived in New York, and began working as Tesla's assistant at tne new laboratory.
  • 1888 - In May, Tesla presented a paper,"A New System of Alternate-Current Motors and Transformers", to the American Institue of Electrical Engineers (now the IEEE) at Columbia University in New York. In July, he sold patents for the Alternate-Current Polyphase System to George Westinghouse for $25,000 in cash, $50,000 in notes, and a royalty of $2.50 per horsepower for each motor. He traveled to Westinghouse in Pittsburgh to finalize the contract and to help develop his motor. He stayed in Pittsburgh for about a year.
  • 1889 - Telsa returned to New York, and locateed his new laboratory at 175 Grand Street. Here, Telsa began to work with high frequency apparatus, wireless transmission, and theories on the relationship between electro-magnetic radiation and light. Later in the year, he visted Paris for the Universal Exposition, and the unveiling of the Eiffel Tower. While he was there, he met Professor Wilhelm Bjerknes, a Norwegian Physicist from the University of Stockholm. Professor Bjerknes had replicated the work of Henrich Hertz, and allowed Telsa to study his oscillator. Also during this time, Tesla visited his homeland, Smiljan.
  • 1890 - In August, as a part of an Edison sponsored smear campaign against Westinghouse, an ally of Edison, who was a manufacturer of electric chairs, began electrocuting animals with Alternating Current. The process became known as "Westinghoused." This was used to demonstrate the dangers of the new technology. Work on the Tesla induction was abandoned, after he failed to adapt to the higher frequency which Westinghouse engineers required. Tesla forfieted the Alternating Current Royality clause of the contract with Westinghouse. This was a fateful decision and a sacrifice that would haunt Tesla for the rest of his life. In November, working with high frequencies Tesla developed some of the first neon and fluorescent illumination. He also took the first x-ray photographs. But these discoveries paled in comparison to his illuminating a vacuum tube, wirelessly. This was the beginning of Tesla's lifelong obsession with the wireless transmission of energy.
  • 1891 - In May, Tesla gave a lecture of "Experiments with Alternate Currents of Very High Frequency and Their Application to Methods of Artifical Illumination" before the American Institue of Electrical Engineers (now the IEEE) at Columbia University in New York. In June, L. L. Nunn, a Colorado lawyer and manager of the Gold King Mine, signed a contract with Westinghouse to install Tesla's Alternating Current Power System. This facility became known as the Ames Power Plant, and was the first power station in the world to transmit alternating current at high voltage for power purposes for a long distance. In July, Tesla became an American citizen. It is reported that Tesla often told friends that he valued this citizenship more than any scientific honors he received. In August, Tesla published the article, "Reply to J. J. Thomson's Note" in the "Electrical Engineer, New York." In this article, he claimed that his experiments prove the existence of charged particles, while J. J. Thomson denied the existence. However, five years later, using another experiment, Thomson proved their existence. Maybe not the most successful of Tesla's inventions, but perhaps the invention he is most famous for is the Tesla Coil. The Tesla Coil was originally developed to power Tesla's new wireless lighting systems, but later became the basis of the ill-fated World-Wide Wireless System. It is better known as Wardenclyffe.
  • 1892 - During February, Tesla gave "Experiments with Alternate Currents of High Potential and High Frequency" lecture to the Institution of Electrical Engineers, The Royal Institution of Great Britian, and the Societe Francaise de Physique. During this time, he received notification that his mother was gravely ill. In April, his mother passed. Upon returning to America, after three years living in Astor House in New York City, he moved to the Hotel Gerlach, which was much more modern hotel. For example, it was equipped with elevators, electric lights, and sumptuous dining rooms. Also, this hotel was fireproof and was located just a few blocks from the newly finished Madison Square Garden. He also became the Vice-President of the AIEE for two consecutive years.
  • 1893 - During February Tesla gave "On Light and Other High Frequency Phenomena" lecture to the Franklin Institute of Philadelphia. And in March, he gave the same lecture to the National Electric Light Association in St. Louis, Missouri. In May, at the Columbian Exposition, Tesla had a large display, which included phosphorescent lighting (a precursor to fluorescent lamps) powered without wires by high-frequency fields and the Egg of Columbus. The success of the Tesla Polyphase System installed at the exposition ensured Westinghouse would be selected to harness the Niagara. Tesla gave the lecture, "Mechanical and Electrical Oscillators" to the International Electrical Congress at the Columbian Exposition in Chicago, Illinois. In October, Westinghouse is awarded the initial Niagara Falls contract, signifying the end of the "The War of the Currents."
  • 1894 - In the spring of the year, Tesla was voted corresponding member of the Serbian Royal Academy of Belgrade. Also during this year, Thomas Commerford Martin, editor of "Electrical World" and Vice-President of the AIEE published the first book of Tesla's research. The book, "The Inventions, Researches and Writing of Nikola Tesla" is a comprehensive compilation of Tesla's work with many illustrations.
  • 1895 - In March, a fire broke out in the basement of 33-35 South 5th Avenue (now West Broadway), and swept through the entire six floor building, including Tesla's laboratory, which occupied the entire fourth floor. All of his hundreds of invention models, plans, notes, laboratory data, tools, and photographs, valued at about $50,000, were destroyed. In April, the first large generator of the Niagara Falls Power Plant, which bore Tesla's name and patent numbers, was ran at full speed, 250 revolution per minute. The result were satisfactory. Thomas Commerford Martin's article, "Tesla's Oscillator and Other Inventions", was published. In December, Tesla had originally noticed what he described as "a very special radiation" years earlier when working his "carbon-button" lamp. He produced pictures he called "shadowgraphs" and had performed numerous experiments with them up until the fire at his lab. Upon learning of Rontgen's discovery of X-Rays, Tesla wrote him and sent some of the pictures recovered from the fire. After the fire destroyed his laboratory, he temporary used Thomas Edison's workshop at Llewellyn Park, New Jersey. Within a few weeks, Tesla rented a laboratory below Greenwich Village. This building is now the home of Soho Billards.
  • 1896 - The new Alternating Current Power System enjoyed a flawless inauguration, transmitting electricity from Niagara to Buffalo, New York, about 22 miles away. During December, Tesla was elected honorary member of the Yugoslav Academy of Sciences and Arts in Zagreb.
  • 1897 - In January, Tesla gave lecture, "On Electricity" for commemoration of the introduction of Niagara Falls Power in Buffalo, New York. In April, he gave lecture, "The Streams of Lenard and Rontgen and Novel Apparatus for Their Productiion" before the New York Academy of Sciences at the American Museum of Natural History building in New York.
  • 1898 - In September, Tesla lectured on "High Frequency Oscillators for Electro-therapeutic and Other Purposes" before the Electro-therapeutic Association at the Buffalo Society of Natural Sciences in Buffalo, New York. In December, he demonstrated his "automation" technology by wirelessly controlling a model boat at the Electrical Exposition held at Madison Square Garden in New York City, during the height of the Spanish-American War.
  • 1899 - In May, Tesla addressed and demonstrated his "Teleautomatics" before the Commercial Club in Chicago. Later in May, he arrived in Colorado Springs to continue high-voltage and high-frequency experiments that had become too large for his New York lab. In June, Tesla began conducting experiments in Colorado Springs. He claimed discovery of a new geo-electrical phenomenon, which he said would allow for the transport of electricity around the world. Terrestrial stationary waves were first observed by Tesla and formed the basis for his wireless energy transfer plans and wireless communications. Upon receiving a $100,000 investment from John Jacob Astor, Tesla moved into the Waldorf-Astoria, which Astor owned. At the time, this was the world's tallest building. Working late one night on his powerful and sensitive radio receiver, Tesla observed strange rhythmic pulses on the receiver. He concluded that there was no possible explanation other than some effort was being made to communicate with Earth by creatures from another planet. Tesla reveals this discovery and is highly criticized.
  • 1900 - In his autobiography, "My Inventions", Tesla states, "When in 1900 I obtained powerful discharges of 100 feet (in the Colorado Springs laboratory) and flashed a current around the globe, I was reminded of the first tiny spark I observed in my Grand St. laboratory and was thrilled by sensations akin to those I felt when I discovered the rotating magnetic field." Tesla formed the Tesla Ozone Company, and went into production for his Corona Discharge Ozone Generators. In 1896, he was issued a patent for this generator, which used charged metal plates to act on ambient air. His customers were naturopaths and allopaths who welcomed this powerful therapy into their practices. Breathing of ozone bubbled through olive oil and other oils was widely practiced at this time, and the Sears catalog of 1904 offered a unit for this purpose using eucalyptus, pine, and spearmint oils. Telsa produced a gel made by bubbling ozone through olive oil until it solidified, and sold it to doctors.
  • 1901 - Tesla partnered with J. P. Morgan, and signed over 51 percent interest in his patents and inventions, including future ones, relating to both electric lighting and wireless telegraphy or telephony for a mere $150,000. In December Guglielmo Marconi, a Tesla rival, succeeds in sending the first radio transmission across the Atlantic Ocean. And Tesla began his most ambitious project, The World-Wide Wireless System known as Wardenclyffee Tower.
  • 1903 - Wardenclyffe Tower began testing. "The New York Sun," for example, reported the strange goings-on at Tesla's transmitter. It stated, "all sorts of lightning were flashed from the tall tower and poles," and "the air was filled with blinding streaks of electricity which seemed to shoot off into the darkness on some mysterious errand."
  • 1904 - The Colorado Springs Power Company sued Tesla for electricity used at Tesla Colorado laboratory. His laboratory was torn down and sold for lumber to the $180 judgment; his electrical equipment was put in storage.
  • 1906 - In June, Stanford White, who designed Tesla's Wardenclyffe laboratory building, was shot and killed while attending the opening of Madison Square Garden's roof show event. He was murdered because of his involvement in a love triangle. In July, in an article published on Tesla's 50th birthday, he announced his bladeless turbine to the world. The invention was based on adhesion and viscosity, two fundamental properties of all fluids. Also, Tesla made great efforts to obtain funding for the Wardenclyffe project after J. P. Morgan withdrew his support. He was unsuccessful and was unable to pay his employees. He was forced to lay them off and construction of the tower ceased. Later in the year, Teslas contracted by Waltham Watch Company to build the world's first and only air-friction speedometer, which he also patented. It was used in Packard, Lincoln, and Pierce-Arrow luxury cars.
  • 1907 - In May, in an article entitled, "Tesla's Tidal Wave To Make War Impossible," from "English Mechanic and World of Science," Tesla stated that his magnifying transmitter had obtained the rate of 25 million horsepower. The New York Academy of Sciences elected Tesla as an active member. Eager to allocate alternate funding for the Wardenclyffe project, Tesla opened an office 165 Broadway, which is now 1 Liberty Plaza. He began to work on other inventions such as propulsion systems and the vertical take-off and landing of aircraft.
  • 1909 - The Nobel Prize for Physics was shared between Guglielmo Marconi and Karl Ferdinand Braun "in recognition of their contributions to the development of wire telegraphy." Tesla was furious and intesified his long battle to correct the injustice.
  • 1910 - In April Mark Twain, a friend, died of a heart attack in Redding, Connecticut, one day after Halley's Comet's closest approach to Earth. Years later, Tesla, near his own death, would attempt to send money to Twain by messenger. In November after gaining some initial success with his turbines, Tesla relocated his to the prestigious 48-story Metropolitan Tower, which was the world's tallest building at the time.
  • 1911 - Over a period of months, Tesla tested numerous models of steam turbines at the Waterside Station of the New York Edison Company. When interviewed about the tests, Tesla stated, "In one of them the disks are only nine inches in diameter and the whole working part is two inches thick. With steam as the propulsive fluid it develops 110 horse power, and could do twice as much."
  • 1912 - John Astor was Tesla's wealthiest and most generous investor. He invested $100,000 in 1899 for Tesla to, as he understood it, further develop and produce a new lighting system. Instead, Tesla used the money to fund his Colorado Spring experiments. Mr. Astor was understandably unhappy with Tesla's deception and avoided him for several years. Later, they did reconcile and worked together on aircraft and propulsion systems in 1908. Unfortunately, Mr. Astor and his wife were aboard the "Titanic" which sank after colliding with an iceberg. Later in the year, Westinghouse, Church, Kerr & Company repossessed the tower equipment as part of their judgment of $23,500 against Tesla.
  • 1914 - George Westinghouse died in New York City, at age 67. The relationship between George Westinghouse and Tesla was later called "The Perfect Partnership", for there is little doubt about the impact Westinghouse and Tesla had up the world. Also during this year, Tesla moved his office from the Metropolitan Tower to the Woolworth Building, at the time the world's tallest building. He fell behind in rent after only a few months and was forced to leave.
  • 1915 - "The New York Times," followed by other prominent news sources, carried a story, based upon a Reuters dispatch from London, stating that Tesla and Edison were to share the Nobel Prize in physics. Both Tesla and Edison told reporters they had received no official notification of the award. A few days later the Nobel Committee announced that the prize for physics would, in fact, be shared by Professor William Henry Bragg of the University of Leeds in England and his son. The committee did not deny that Tesla and Edison were the first choices, but never made public the true reason for the change. Some speculate that either Tesla or Edison or perhaps both refused to share the prize with the other. After being forced to leave the Woolworth Building, Tesla relocates his office to 8W 40th Street, which was directly across the street from Bryant Park and his beloved pigeons. In order to put a roof over his head, Tesla gave two mortgages on Wardenclyffe Tower to George C. Boldt, proprietor of the Waldorf-Astoria, to secure payment of hotel bills amounting to almost $19,000. Fearing that all his projects would be destroyed if the matter became public, Tesla requested that they not be recorded. He was unable to make any payments at all, and was forced to sign the deed over to Waldorf-Astoria, Inc., through a silent intermediary.
  • 1916 - Even though Tesla had some minor successes, he continued to fall deeper and deeper into debt primarily due to his high overhead. He was called into the State Supreme Court for $935.00 in unpaid taxes on the Wardenclyffee property. Under oath before Justice Finch, Tesla revealed that he was essentially penniless and owned no real assets. He declared bankruptcy.
  • 1917 - In May, after originally rejecting the offer of the AIEE's highest award, Tesla reconsidered and accepted, after being encourage by Bernard A. Behrend. During the introduction, Tesla disappeared and was later found at the library feeding his beloved pigeons. He was persuaded to return and gave his acceptance speech. In July, reported in the media as being suspected of being used by German spies, Wardenclyffe Tower was actually ordered to be destroyed to cover debts incurred by Tesla at the Waldorf-Astoria, where he lived for almost 20 years. He contracted with Pyle National on the perfection of his turbines. He moved to Chicago, and lived in the Blackstone Hotel where he stayed for the next 16 months. In the August edition of "Electrical Experimenter" magazine, Tesla stated, "For instance by their (standing electromagnetic waves) use we may produce at will, from a sending station, an electrical effect in any particular region of the globe; (with which) we may determine the relative position or course of a moving object, such as a vessel at sea, the distance traversed by the same, or its speed." Tesla envisioned radar.
  • 1918 - Upon returning to New York from Chicago, tesla moved into the Hotel St. Regis, one of the city most exclusive hotels.
  • 1919 - The "Electrical Experimenter" magazine began a series of articles entitled, "My Inventions" by Tesla. The articles were later converted to a book with the same title.
  • 1922 - Tesla had many pigeons he fed and cared for; but one, he was particularly fond of. He described it as being a beautiful femal bird, pure white with light gray tips on the wings. One night the bird flew into Tesla's room at the Hotel St. Regis, and he preceived that she was attempting to tell him she was dying. Tesla said a light came from her eyes more intense than he had ever produced by the most powerful lamps in his laboratory. The bird then died and Tesla said that at that same moment, something went out of his life and he knew his life's work was finished.
  • 1923 - Tesla was charged $15.00 per day in rent, an exorbitant amount for the time, while staying at the St. Regis. After not paying anything toward a balance of over $3,000 he ran up over a seven-month period, he was forced to leave. Always seeking lavishness, Tesla moved to the luxurious Hotel Marguery on the west side of Park Avenue between 47th and 48th.
  • 1924 - In May, Tesla left the Hotel Regis owing a debt of over $3,000. After having moved out over six months earlier and never making an effort to resolve the debt, Tesla was sued for the balance. Shortly afterward, a sheriff's deputy was sent to Tesla's office at 8W 40th Street to seize furnishings in order to satisfy the debt. Tesla managed to persuade the officer to grant him an extension. Late in the year, Tesla meet Ivan Mestrovic, a internationally renowned Croatian sculptor. They became good friends and admirers of each other, and later exchanged numerous letters.
  • 1925 - For reasons unknown, Tesla rented another room at the Hotel Pennsylvania and made it his primary residence while continuing to rent the room at the Hotel Marguery. He confided in Kenneth Swezey that the room at Hotel Marguery was for meeting "special" friends and acquaintances. According to some accounts, the only woman Tesla ever loved was Katharine McMahon Johnson. She was the wife of Tesla's lifelong friend, Robert Underwood Johnson. Though the two, Katharine and Tesla, often exchanged flirtatious letters, their relationship was totally platonic. She died. Not forgetting Tesla even at death, she charged her husband to keep in close touch with him always. For unknown reasons, Tesla relocated his office from 8W 40th Street to 350 Madison Avenue. By 1928 the maintenance of an office will become too burdensome, and it will be closed for good.
  • 1926 - In June, Tesla received a diploma of honorary doctorate from the Faculty of Engineering, University of Belgrade. Along this time, he received another honorary doctorate from the University of Zagreb. In New York, Tesla met Sara N. Kosanovic, the son of Tesla's younger sister, Marica Kosanovic. Mr. Sara Kosanovic was a diplomat carrying the title of Ambassador of the Federal People's Republic of Yugoslavia. During his visit to New York, it was his first meeting of his Uncle Tesla.
  • 1930 - After residents of the hotel complained about the droppings from his "flying rats" (pigeons) and because he was $2,000 behind in his rent, Tesla was asked to leave the Hotel Pennsylvania. Tesla's friend and admirer, B. A. Behrend helped with the debt and Tesla relocated to the Hotel Governor Clinton.
  • 1931 - On his 75th birthday, Tesla received many congratulatory letters from prominent scientists, including Albert Einstein. In July, "Time" magazine honored Tesla by placing his portrait on the cover. In October, Tesla's most prominent adversary, Thomas Edison, died at the age of 84.
  • 1934 - In January, a settlement was reached with the Westinghouse Corportation that provided Tesla with a consulting rate of $125.00 per month along with the agreement to pay his monthly rent expenses. Upon signing the agreement, Telsa promptly moved to the Hotel New Yorker, where he lived rent free for the rest of his days. The Hotel Governor Clinton debt was never paid. In March, Tesla wrote an article called "Possibilities of Electro-Static Generators" was published in the "Scientific American." It featured "Colossus," a two million volt Van de Graff generator, which is now on display at the Boston Museum of Science. In June, the City of Philadelphia awared Tesla with the John Scott Medal for the invention of the rotating magnetic field and induction motor. In July, "The New York Times" reported about Tesla's Death Ray. The article stated that the new invention "will send concentrated beans of particles throught the free air, of such tremendous energy that they will bring down a fleet of 10,000 enemy airpalances at a distance of 250 miles..." Tesla stated that the death beam would make war impossible by offering every country an "invisible Chinese wall."
  • 1935 - In a "Liberty" magazine article entitled, "A Machine to End War," G. S. Viereck, a friend of Tesla and a Nazi spokesperson, reported on what Tesla believed the world would be like in the years 2035 and 2100.
  • 1937 - In January Tesla received a certificate of honorary doctorate from the University of Poiters. Tesla was the first scientist nominated for permanent membership of the Servian Royal Academy of Sciences, but was not initially elected. In March, when he was notified that the was elected for full membership in this renowned institution, Tesla did not even respond to the telegram. They asked him to send his curriculm vitae in order to print it in ther annual, the annual was published without his text.
  • 1938 - In May, in response to an honorary citation, a speech (delivered in absentia due to illness) was given before a meeting of the Institute of Immigrant Welfare, Biltmore Hotel, New York.
  • 1942 - In his diaries "A King's Heritage," the young Peter II wrote: "I visited Dr. Nikola Tesla, in his apartment in the Hotel New Yorker. After I had greeted him, the aged scientisit said, "It is my greatest honor. I am glad you are in your youth, and I am content that you will be a great ruler. I believe I will live until you come back to a free Yugoslavia. From your father you have received his last words: "Guard Yugoslavia." I am proud to be a Serbian and a Yugoslav. Our people cannot perish. Preserve the unity of all Yugoslavs - the Serbs, the Croats, and Slovenes."
  • 7 January 1943 - Tesla died quietly and alone in room #3327 on the 33rd floor of the Hotel New York in New York City. The coroner estimated the time of death at 22:30 EST. He was 86 years old. It is reported that Tesla had placed a "do not disturb" sign on his door on January 5, 1943. Ignoring the sign, the maid entered the room to find him dead in his bed. After medical examination of the body, the cause of death was determined be coronary thrombosis and that there had been no suspicious circumstances.
  • Closing Thoughts - In many ways, Telsa was an eccentric genius. His discoveries and inventions were unprecedented. However, he was misunderstood and looked at with suspect for his obsessive compulsive behavior. For he was not afraid to suggest unorthodox ideas, such as receiving radio waves from extraterrestrial beings; his ideals, lack of personal finance, and nonconformist behavior put him outside of the scientific establishment. And because of this, his ideas were sometimes slow to be accepted or used.
